**origin:**

- A short Greek genealogical line from the biblical tradition, most likely the genealogy in the Gospel of Matthew (showing the Davidic/Judean royal line: Hezekiah → Manasseh → Amon → Josiah). The wording resembles Matthew 1:10; the form Ἀμὼς appears like a copying/orthographic slip for the expected Ἀμὼν (Amon).

**author:**

- Most likely the author/compiler traditionally associated with the Gospel of Matthew (i.e., “Matthew” as understood in early Christian tradition), or more generally a Greek-scripture scribe/translator preserving Matthew’s text.

**thoughts:**

- The author’s perspective is doctrinal and legitimizing: the genealogy is included to confirm that the promised Messiah fits the expected line (Davidic descent) and that sacred history forms a coherent fulfillment of God’s plan. The author likely intends the reader to see continuity from the respected kings of Judah to the later figure at the center of the Gospel.

**audience:**

- Early Jewish or Jewish-Christian readers (and secondarily Greek-speaking Christians) who are interested in Jesus’ identity and messianic credentials—particularly readers who value lineage, Scripture fulfillment, and royal/Davidic legitimacy.

**written_when:**

- Late 1st century CE (roughly 80–90 CE is a common scholarly estimate for Matthew’s final form). The Greek language also fits this period of manuscript transmission; it could have been copied/transmitted later as well, but the underlying composition is typically dated to that late 1st-century window.

**notes:**

- The Greek uses the formula ἐγέννησεν (“begot/fathered,” i.e., “was the father of”) and lists a genealogy in sequence. The name Ἀμὼς is the main potential ambiguity: it likely corresponds to Amon (the king), even though it is spelled like “Amos.”
