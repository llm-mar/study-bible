## Author Analysis

**origin:**

- A Hebrew Bible passage about the “suffering servant” (often identified with Isaiah 53:12): the text frames a reversal in fate—someone who bore many sins and faced death is instead promised a reward, including a share of “spoils” and participation alongside the “great,” implying divine vindication after sacrifice.

**author:**

- Most likely an anonymous prophetic writer/redactor within the Isaiah tradition (commonly attributed to “Second Isaiah” for Isaiah 40–55, though exact authorship is debated). In interpretive traditions, Christians frequently associate the servant figure with Jesus and therefore treat the passage as messianic prophecy; Jews generally treat it within broader servant/people themes rather than a direct messianic identification.

**thoughts:**

- The author’s perspective appears to be theological and providential: death is not meaningless but is portrayed as effective atonement—“because he poured out his life to death… and was numbered with the transgressors.” The motivation seems to be to comfort a suffering community and to assert that God ultimately justifies the seemingly condemned figure, culminating in honor, restoration, and moral accounting (atonement leading to vindication).

**audience:**

- Primarily an ancient Judean/Israelite audience reading prophetic scripture in a period of hardship—people needing hope that suffering will be reversed and that divine justice will prevail. In later history, it also strongly targets religious communities engaged in messianic/atonement interpretation (notably Christian communities).

**written_when:**

- Likely during the late Babylonian exile or shortly after, roughly the 6th century BCE (often dated to the era of Second Isaiah), though the text’s reception and later interpretive use span many centuries beyond its original composition.

## Translation Analysis

**translation:**

- Therefore I will allot him a portion with the many, and with the mighty he shall divide the spoil—because he poured out his life unto death and was counted among the transgressors; and he bore the sin of many, and for the transgressors he will intercede.
