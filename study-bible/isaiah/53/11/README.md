## Author Analysis

**origin:**

- A Hebrew biblical verse from the “Suffering Servant” passage, commonly identified as Isaiah 53:11 (often cited in Jewish and Christian traditions as part of a prophecy about a righteous servant who suffers and bears others’ iniquities). The text appears to be a scriptural/translation-style rendering with cantillation/accents (e.g., the ֤֣֔֗ marks).

**author:**

- Traditionally, attributed to the prophet Isaiah (or to an Isaiah-author figure/redactor within the Isaiah tradition). Many modern scholars instead treat Isaiah 53 as coming from a later author/editor (exilic or post-exilic context), while still preserving “Isaiah” as the attributed source in the book.

**thoughts:**

- The perspective is theological and redemptive: the speaker frames the servant’s suffering as meaningful rather than wasted. The motivation is to offer vindication (“he will see… he will be satisfied with knowledge”) and to explain moral/atoning purpose—“the righteous servant justifies many” and “he bears their iniquities.” The author likely wants to sustain hope for a community enduring injustice by portraying suffering as ultimately transformative and restorative.

**audience:**

- An ancient Israelite/Judahite audience dealing with oppression and moral crisis; later readers in Jewish worship and in Christian communities (especially those interpreting Isaiah 53 as foreshadowing the Messiah) would also see this as a devotional or doctrinal text.

**written_when:**

- Most likely in the late monarchic to exilic/post-exilic period—commonly dated to around the 6th century BCE (traditional linkage to Isaiah spans ~8th–7th century BCE, but the specific “Servant” material is frequently placed closer to exilic/post-exilic times).

**translation:**
